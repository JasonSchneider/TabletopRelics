## Overview

This adventure introduces the **Haunted Lantern**. The lantern is *not* the antagonist; it is a witness. The party meets it during an investigation, and by the end of the night they will have decided whether to claim it, destroy it, or leave it to keep its lonely watch.

This module leans on atmosphere over combat. There is one optional fight at the end, but the heart of the night is what the lantern *shows* the players, not what it does.

> Read aloud:
> Caedmon's Yard is the smallest of the city's three burial grounds. Twenty stones, a bent yew tree, a wrought iron gate that hasn't latched right since the war. By day, it is unremarkable. By night, it has begun to glow — the way breath glows in the cold — and the glow follows people home.

## Hooks for the DM

The party is hired by **Mira Veck**, a sexton who has been blamed for the trouble. Her wages have been suspended, her sleep ruined. She offers 50 gp from her own purse if the party will spend a single night in the yard and tell her honestly what they saw.

A subtler hook: a child the party knows has begun sleepwalking, and the child wakes up holding a single dry leaf — a leaf from the yew tree at Caedmon's Yard, which they have never visited.

## Scene 1 — The Sexton's Shed

Mira's shed is the obvious starting point. She is exhausted, frightened, and *not* the source of the haunting — let the party verify this and feel clever for doing so. She gives them three pieces of information:

1. The lights began on the night of the **Quartzday** funeral, two weeks ago.
2. They follow only certain people. She cannot say which.
3. There is one grave the lights *never* approach: a small unmarked stone in the corner under the yew.

**The unmarked stone** is the seed of the mystery. Mira does not know whose it is.

## Scene 2 — Walking the Yard

The party can spend an evening among the stones. The lantern reveals itself slowly. It is not yet a physical object — only a small green flame that hangs in mid-air, about three feet off the ground, near the yew tree.

> Read aloud:
> A flame. Pale green, as small as a child's fist, hovering exactly where no candle could be. As you watch, it dips toward you — not a wind, not a person, but a *gesture* — and waits.

**Approaching the flame.** Whoever steps closest may attempt a **Wisdom (Insight) DC 13**.

- **Success:** They feel grief, but it is not their own. The flame is mourning *for* someone. They get a name: **Tess.**
- **Failure:** They feel watched, and have disadvantage on their next save against being frightened tonight.

## Scene 3 — The Procession

Once acknowledged, the flame begins to walk. It moves at a slow stroll — the speed of a funeral procession — and leads the party past every stone in the yard, dipping briefly in front of each. At the unmarked stone in the corner, it stops.

A **Wisdom (Perception) DC 14** notices that the dirt at the base of the stone has been recently disturbed. A short dig reveals a small lacquered box.

**Inside the box:** a child's drawing of a woman in a red apron, a chipped clay whistle, and a single dry yew leaf.

The flame, when shown the contents, **brightens** — and then, for the first time, takes on form: a small iron lantern, wrought with vines, with the green flame burning inside. It hovers a moment, then settles into the hands of whoever holds out the box.

> Read aloud:
> The flame does not enter the lantern so much as *remember* it — the way a name returns to a face you almost forgot. The brass is cool. The flame is warm. It does not flicker. It waits.

## Scene 4 — The Optional Confrontation

If you want a combat beat: as the party leaves the yard with the lantern, they are accosted by **three caretakers from a rival cemetery** who have been bribed by an aristocrat to "remove the disturbance" — meaning, smash the lantern. They are thugs, not monsters: **AC 12, HP 18, two attacks at +3 for 1d6+1 each**. They can be talked down with a successful **Charisma (Persuasion) DC 14** if the party reveals what the lantern *is*.

If the lantern is held during this fight, the players may notice that its flame **brightens around lies and dims around truths spoken under duress** — a trait they can exploit.

## Aftermath

The lantern is now bonded to whoever holds it. Burying the box at any other grave releases that grave's grief into the flame, and the lantern flickers in patterns that are *almost* speech. From here on, the **`lantern.trigger`** commands (`flicker`, `ghost`, `wind`, `snuff`) become diegetic — the lantern responding to the world, not the player.

Mira pays the party. The unmarked stone, by morning, has a name carved on it: **Tess Aldery, who was kind**.

## Adapting

- **Lower stakes:** Cut the rival caretakers and end on the box. The session becomes a quiet ghost story.
- **Campaign seed:** Tess was murdered. The lantern can identify her killer — but only if the party teaches it to recognize the killer's voice, which means meeting them first.
- **Horror tone:** The flame is *not* Tess. It is something pretending to be her, and the box was bait.
